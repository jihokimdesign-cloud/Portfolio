Beyond Chatbots: Designing Emotional Continuity for Gen Z

Most wellness apps speak. Few listen. We built one that does.

Lepal.aiThe ProblemResearchThe FrameworkExecutionCollaborationOutcomeReflection

Why We Built Lepal.ai

Existing wellness solutions offered productivity hacks but lacked emotional continuity. Target users — Gen Z — needed empathy, not efficiency. They wanted a space that remembered their feelings, not just their tasks.

The “72-Hour Wall”: Most wellness apps see a sharp drop-off after 3 days. Users start enthusiastic, but the generic responses erode trust quickly. By day 3, the app feels like talking to a stranger.

As Lead Designer, I owned end-to-end design for 3 core experience pillars: emotional journaling, adaptive AI tone, and long-term mood continuity.

Validating the Hypothesis

Conducted 5 semi-structured interviews with Gen Z graduate students paired with a 3-day mood diary study. The goal: understand not just what users said, but how their emotional needs shifted over time.

  • Users wanted the app to 'remember' previous conversations
  • Generic positive affirmations felt dismissive during low moods
  • Journaling worked best when it felt like writing to a friend, not a form
  • Privacy concerns were high — users needed to trust the AI wouldn't judge

Adaptive Tone Framework

Reframed the goal from “Fixing memory” to “Prioritizing empathy before solutions.” Built an Emotion-Aware AI Framework in collaboration with ML engineers and the PM.

  • Mood detection layer: Sentiment analysis on journal entries
  • Adaptive tone engine: Responses shift from supportive to celebratory based on context
  • Memory threads: AI references past entries to create continuity
  • Escalation protocol: Detects crisis signals and adjusts response appropriately

Designing “My Journal”

Led the entire ecosystem design — from the journal entry flow to the AI conversation interface to the mood timeline visualization. The core challenge: making AI feel like a supportive companion, not a robot.

Turning Conflict into Co-Creation

The biggest challenge wasn't the design — it was aligning the team on design philosophy. Engineers wanted efficiency metrics; I advocated for emotional outcomes. Through regular workshops and shared research artifacts, we found common ground.

From Productivity Tool to Emotional Companion

A/B tested “Productivity-driven” vs. “Empathy-first” tone. The empathy-first approach won across every metric.

What I Learned

The best AI products understand context, not just language. Designing for emotional continuity requires a fundamentally different approach than designing for task completion.

  • Empathy is a design material, not just a value
  • AI tone must be adaptive — one-size-fits-all responses erode trust
  • Long-term retention comes from emotional safety, not feature novelty
Good product design is about designing relationships, not just interfaces. The AI that listens first and suggests second is the one users come back to.

Good design is invisible. Great design is felt.
