ProxiPlay: An Adaptive UI for Hands-Occupied Cooking

When voice and gestures fail, the screen adapts to your elbow.

72% of cooking video users pause to wash hands before tapping “Next”

Recipe video apps assume clean, dry fingers — but users are elbow-deep in flour, raw meat, or wet ingredients. The standard touch interface fails in the kitchen's most critical moments.

Understanding hands-occupied interaction patterns

Combined three research methods — in-home observation, sensor data analysis, and structured interviews — to map the real interaction patterns of people cooking with video guidance.

Key insight: Kitchen noise levels regularly exceed 70dB during active cooking — well above the threshold where voice recognition becomes unreliable. Users need physical interaction modalities that don't require clean hands.
  • In-home observation of 8 participants cooking with video apps
  • Sensor data from accelerometer and proximity sensors during cooking tasks
  • Semi-structured interviews focused on workaround behaviors

Adaptive UI State Machine

Designed a multi-signal trigger system that gracefully degrades from standard touch input to proximity-based macro interaction. The system detects hand proximity and automatically enlarges tap targets, simplifying the interface to essential navigation controls.

What I learned

The biggest takeaway: instead of adding input modalities, rethink the interaction pattern itself. The solution wasn't better voice recognition or gesture detection — it was adapting the entire UI to the user's physical state.

  • Context-aware UI beats adding more input modalities
  • Test in realistic conditions — lab settings miss critical friction
  • Graceful degradation > feature switches for accessibility
Instead of adding more input modalities, the real breakthrough came from listening to what's already failing — and responding to that context.
