While I designed the entire ecosystem, the biggest challenge lay in My Journal, where the AI needed to feel like a supportive companion, not a robot. Getting there is the story of this case study.

<SectionTitle num="03" kicker="Strategy" title="Teaching the AI to read the room" />

I reframed the goal from "fixing memory" to "prioritizing empathy before solutions," and collaborated with ML engineers and the PM on an emotion-aware framework: instead of one-size-fits-all replies, the system classifies every input into one of four emotional states — anxious, sad, neutral, positive — and adapts its tone, structure, and pacing to match.

<SectionTitle num="04" kicker="Design" title="Two decisions that made it feel human" />

### Decision 01 — The thinking indicator

Even with an empathetic tone, instant responses felt robotic. We needed a cue that implied care, not loading.

<TradeOff
  options={[
    {
      label: "A",
      title: "Generic indicator",
      text: "“Generating response...” — clear system status, easy to build.",
      verdict: "Too functional. Feels like waiting for a server, not a friend.",
    },
    {
      label: "B",
      title: "Typing dots",
      text: "“Thinking...” — mimics human behavior.",
      verdict: "Feels like a companion is thinking about what you said.",
      selected: true,
    },
  ]}
/>

I shipped the dots with variable latency — 1–1.5s for light check-ins, up to 2–3s for heavy emotional sharing — shifting perception from "the app is downloading a reply" to "someone is thinking about me."

### Decision 02 — The blank page dilemma

Users froze at an empty chat box, but permanent FAQ guidance clutters the chat and biases input. The hybrid: contextual starters on first run only ("My head is a mess from labs and assignments 🤯"), fading out once a user engages, with a discreet "i" button as the safety net.

Neither decision was mine to ship alone — both meant convincing people whose instincts said the opposite.

<SectionTitle num="05" kicker="Collaboration" title="“Latency isn't lag — it's listening”" />

When I proposed variable latency, the lead engineer pushed back: **"You want us to add artificial latency?"** The line above was my answer — but it only landed because we tested it. Against an instant-reply control, the 1.5–3s variant scored significantly higher on "feeling heard" with **zero complaints about speed**. Variable latency shipped as a feature, not a bug.

The same pattern resolved the "friction vs. data" debate with the PM: starters on for new users, off for returning ones, FAQ button as the safety net. Retention protected, data quality intact.

The Tone Framework became the team's shared language — design owned tone rules, engineering owned latency logic, the PM owned metrics, the conversation designer owned empathy markers.

<SectionTitle num="06" kicker="Outcome" title="From productivity tool to companion" />

We ran a pre-launch A/B test: the original productivity-driven tone as control, the emotion-aware framework as variant. The variant won on every measure — retention, engagement, satisfaction, and how "supportive" users rated the AI — and shipped in the launched mobile app.

<SectionTitle num="07" kicker="Reflection" title="What I learned" />

-- Good product design is about designing relationships.

I aligned Engineering's need for logic with Design's need for empathy by building a shared framework they could both contribute to — conflict became co-creation. And success wasn't about adding more tools: a smaller, warmer feature set won over a larger, colder one.

The best AI products don't just process language — they understand context, respect emotion, and create space for human vulnerability.
