-- When voice and gestures fail, the screen adapts to your elbow.

Recipe video apps assume clean, dry fingers. In reality, users are elbow-deep in flour, raw meat, or wet ingredients. As Interaction Designer and Prototyper, I designed a multi-signal adaptive UI that detects when cooks can't use standard inputs — and switches itself to elbow-operable targets.

<SectionTitle num="01" kicker="The Problem" title="Wash, dry, tap, repeat" />

Voice failed most during the noisiest phases — exactly when hands-free control mattered most. Gesture confidence collapsed with flour, mitts, or utensils in hand. But the most surprising finding was behavioral: **nine of twelve participants** were already trying to tap with their elbows. The 44px targets were just too small for it to work.

<Insight>
  The users had already designed the solution. We just had to build an interface big enough for it.
</Insight>

<SectionTitle num="03" kicker="The System" title="An interface that listens to failure" />

The design responds to failure instead of demanding new behavior. A multi-signal AND-gate watches three conditions — only when all three trip does the UI switch:

<Flow
  nodes={[
    { label: "Trigger 1", title: "Voice input failed", state: "fail" },
    { label: "Trigger 2", title: "Gesture confidence < 40%", state: "fail" },
    { label: "Sensor", title: "Proximity < 30cm", state: "pass" },
    { label: "State change", title: "Elbow-Bump Mode", note: "bottom 50% becomes one giant target", state: "result" },
  ]}
  separator="+"
  caption="The AND-gate — two failed inputs plus an approaching user. Any single signal alone changes nothing."
/>

Four design decisions shaped the final system, each driven by a failure observed in the field:

<List>
  <ListItem label="AND-gate">A single trigger produced 23% false positives; requiring all three dropped it under 3%.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="120× target">We measured real elbow contact areas (4.2 × 6.8cm) before sizing the half-screen zone.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="Overlay">An 85% gradient keeps the recipe visible beneath the button — users never lose their place.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="Auto-revert">8 seconds without proximity reverts to standard controls; double-tap force-exits.</ListItem>
</List>

<SectionTitle num="04" kicker="Validation" title="Testing with flour-covered hands" />

A controlled study with 18 participants cooking real recipes — the same 8-step recipe twice, standard UI vs. adaptive mode. Hand-wash interruptions fell from 6.2 to 0.5 per recipe, transitions dropped from 15.2s to 3.3s, and elbow taps landed 97% of the time.

<SectionTitle num="06" kicker="Reflection" title="Failure is an input" />

-- The real breakthrough came from listening to what's already failing.

The pattern isn't kitchen-specific. A living room where the remote is lost and voice is misheard, an industrial floor where gloves block touch and machinery kills voice — any environment where hands, voice, or attention are compromised benefits from the same AND-gate logic. It's now my default.
