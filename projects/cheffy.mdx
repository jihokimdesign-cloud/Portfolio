-- When voice and gestures fail, the screen adapts to your elbow.

Recipe video apps assume clean, dry fingers. In reality, users are elbow-deep in flour, raw meat, or wet ingredients. As Founding Designer leading interaction and AI prototyping, I designed a multi-signal adaptive UI that detects when cooks can't use standard inputs — and switches itself to elbow-operable targets.

Voice failed most during the noisiest phases — exactly when hands-free control mattered most. Gesture confidence collapsed with flour, mitts, or utensils in hand. But the most surprising finding was behavioral: **nine of twelve participants** were already trying to tap with their elbows. The 44px targets were just too small for it to work.

<Insight>
  The users had already designed the solution. We just had to build an interface big enough for it.
</Insight>

<SectionTitle num="03" kicker="The System" title="An interface that listens to failure" />

The design responds to failure instead of demanding new behavior. A multi-signal AND-gate watches three conditions — only when all three trip does the UI switch:

<Flow
  nodes={[
    { label: "Trigger 1", title: "Voice input failed", state: "fail" },
    { label: "Trigger 2", title: "Gesture confidence < 40%", state: "fail" },
    { label: "Sensor", title: "Proximity < 30cm", state: "pass" },
    { label: "State change", title: "Elbow-Bump Mode", note: "bottom 50% becomes one giant target", state: "result" },
  ]}
  separator="+"
  caption="The AND-gate — two failed inputs plus an approaching user. Any single signal alone changes nothing."
/>

### Decision 01 — Why three signals, not one

The first prototype switched modes on proximity alone — and it was unusable. Leaning in to read a step, checking the pan, reaching past the tablet: nearly one in four activations was false, and every false switch taught users to distrust the mode. It took three prototype rounds to earn the gate:

<TradeOff
  options={[
    {
      label: "Round 1",
      title: "Proximity alone",
      text: "Switch the moment the user comes within 30cm — simplest to build, instant response.",
      verdict: "23% false activations. The UI flipped every time someone leaned in just to read.",
    },
    {
      label: "Round 3",
      title: "The AND-gate",
      text: "Voice failed + gesture confidence below 40% + proximity under 30cm. All three, or nothing.",
      verdict: "Under 3% false activations. The UI changes only when the user demonstrably can't use standard inputs.",
      selected: true,
    },
  ]}
/>

The gate is conservative on purpose: a missed activation costs one hand-wash, but a false one costs trust in the whole mode.

### Decision 02 — Sizing for a body part nobody designs for

Touch guidelines assume an 8–12mm fingertip. An elbow lands as a 40–60mm blob, with far less aim — so before sizing anything, we measured real elbow contact areas (4.2 × 6.8cm) and forearm approach angles. That's why the target is the entire bottom half of the screen rather than a bigger button: roughly 120× the area of a standard 44px target, reachable without looking.

Two more decisions rounded out the system:

<List>
  <ListItem label="Overlay">An 85% gradient keeps the recipe visible beneath the button — users never lose their place.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="Auto-revert">8 seconds without proximity reverts to standard controls; double-tap force-exits.</ListItem>
</List>

<SectionTitle num="06" kicker="Reflection" title="Failure is an input" />

-- The real breakthrough came from listening to what's already failing.

The pattern isn't kitchen-specific. A living room where the remote is lost and voice is misheard, an industrial floor where gloves block touch and machinery kills voice — any environment where hands, voice, or attention are compromised benefits from the same AND-gate logic.

-- Context is the best input. When everything else fails, the system already knows what to do — that's now my default.
