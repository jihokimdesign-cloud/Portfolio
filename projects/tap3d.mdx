-- Learners were quietly quitting a VR training platform. I designed the AI layer that brought them back.

TAP3D prepares workers for advanced manufacturing roles across VR and Web. As the sole product designer on a six-person team, I took an AI agent from risky idea to funded roadmap — by simulating it with 24 real users before a single model was wired up.

None of this existed six months earlier. Here's how it got funded.

<SectionTitle num="01" kicker="The Problem" title="Silent churn, module by module" />

Internal data revealed a significant lag in the training completion cycle — directly impacting customer retention metrics.

The quantitative data showed where learners dropped off. It couldn't show why.

<SectionTitle num="02" kicker="Research" title="9 interviews. 3 barriers." />

I conducted nine in-depth interviews and affinity-mapped 200+ coded quotes around two questions: what triggers a user to start right now, and what's the minimum information needed to commit?

<Insight>
  Learners weren't lazy — starting felt expensive. Every barrier was a cost the design could remove.
</Insight>

<SectionTitle num="04" kicker="Validation" title="So I became the AI" />

The MVP solved "see the task list" — but not "what should I do first," "I'm stuck," or "what did I do last week." The agent could solve all three, but it existed only in mockups, and mockups don't win budget conversations.

For a **Wizard of Oz simulation with 24 users**, I played the agent's backend in real time — reading uploads, drafting handoffs, recommending next steps — while the team observed real behavior with a product that didn't exist yet.

<List>
  <ListItem label="33→71%">Issue handoff — upload a recording, AI drafts the instructor message. Under 2 minutes vs. 24+ hours.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="38→63%">Task start rate — one recommended 5–10 minute action with a one-line rationale.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="25→58%">Next-step acceptance — weekly summaries pair progress with one concrete suggestion.</ListItem>
</List>

The simulation also caught a design flaw live: users added checklist items but didn't start them, because oldest-first sorting buried anything new. I flipped to newest-first with a "New" indicator between test rounds — task starts jumped immediately.

<SectionTitle num="05" kicker="Outcome" title="The CFO said yes" />

I presented AI Agent screens side-by-side against MVP screens to Engineering, the PM, and the CFO. The CFO's concern was ROI and development cost.

<Insight label="The turning point">
  Numbers from a simulation did what mockups couldn't — they made the ROI conversation concrete.
</Insight>

<SectionTitle num="06" kicker="Collaboration" title="One designer, six partners" />

As the sole designer, collaboration wasn't a phase — it was embedded in every step:

<List>
  <ListItem label="Wk 1–3">Research — co-planned interview scripts with the PM; weekly affinity-map syncs with engineering.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="Wk 4">Strategy — engineering flagged latency and token costs early, shaping the phased rollout.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="Wk 5–8">Design — bi-weekly Figma critiques; QA reviewed empty states, errors, and network failures.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="Wk 9–12">Validation — the PM observed WoZ sessions live and helped catch the sort-order issue.</ListItem>
  <ListItem label="Wk 13–24">Ship & scale — co-built the reusable AI widget; defined CN localization and CJK reflow rules.</ListItem>
</List>

<SectionTitle num="07" kicker="Reflection" title="What I'd carry forward" />

-- Simulation testing seeded a culture of data-driven design at TAP3D.

Pre-launch simulation wasn't common there before this project — proving its value to the C-suite changed how the company validates ideas. It also taught me that a phased MVP isn't a compromise; it's the path that makes the ambitious version fundable.
